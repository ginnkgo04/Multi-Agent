Basic Facts & Characteristics

The red panda (Ailurus fulgens) is a small arboreal mammal native to the eastern Himalayas and southwestern China. Despite its name, it is not closely related to the giant panda; its closest relatives are raccoons, weasels, and skunks.

  • Size: Slightly larger than a domestic cat (50-64 cm body length).
  • Weight: 3 to 6.2 kg (6.6 to 13.7 lb).
  • Lifespan: 8-10 years in the wild, up to 15 years in captivity.
  • Distinctive Features: Rusty red fur, white face markings, ringed tail, and semi-retractable claws.
  • Behavior: Primarily crepuscular (active at dawn and dusk) and solitary.

Habitat & Distribution

Red pandas inhabit temperate forests of the Himalayas, ranging from Nepal in the west to China in the east. They are found at elevations between 2,200 and 4,800 meters (7,200 and 15,700 ft).

Their preferred habitat is cool, moist bamboo forests with dense understory and old trees for shelter. They are highly adapted to an arboreal lifestyle, spending most of their time in trees where they sleep, sunbathe, and escape predators.

Threat: Habitat loss and fragmentation due to deforestation, agriculture, and human development are the primary threats to their survival.

Diet & Feeding Habits

Red pandas are primarily herbivorous, with bamboo making up 85-95% of their diet. However, they are classified as carnivores and will occasionally eat eggs, birds, insects, and small mammals.

They have a modified wrist bone that acts like a "false thumb," helping them grasp bamboo stems. Despite this adaptation, they digest bamboo poorly and must consume large quantities (up to 20% of their body weight daily) to get enough nutrients.

Conservation Status & Efforts

Endangered Species

The red panda is classified as Endangered on the IUCN Red List, with an estimated population of fewer than 10,000 mature individuals in the wild, and the population continues to decline.

Major Threats

  • Habitat loss and fragmentation
  • Poaching and illegal pet trade
  • Climate change affecting bamboo forests
  • Disease and inbreeding in small populations
